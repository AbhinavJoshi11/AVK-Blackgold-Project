Where does bitumen come from?

The origins of bitumen and how it reaches the modern road.

By AVK Blackgold · 12 June 2026

Bitumen is a thick, dark, sticky material that binds roads together and keeps them waterproof. It is one of the oldest engineering materials known — natural deposits and seeps were used for waterproofing and construction thousands of years ago.

Most bitumen used today comes from crude oil. At a refinery, crude is heated and separated into fractions; the heaviest fraction left after lighter products (like petrol and diesel) are removed is processed further into bitumen. Because it is the residue of refining, its properties depend on the crude source and the processing route.

Once produced, bitumen is graded by hardness and performance, then transported hot or as a water-based emulsion to the road. There it coats aggregate to form asphalt, or is sprayed as a binder in surface treatments.

Modern road work increasingly modifies bitumen — with polymers such as Kraton SBS, or by emulsifying it — to improve durability, reduce energy use and extend pavement life.
