How bitumen is made: the refinery process

A simple explanation of how bitumen is produced at the refinery.

By AVK Blackgold · 12 June 2026

Bitumen is the heavy residue left after lighter fractions are distilled from crude oil. Making it consistent and fit for roads, however, takes several refining steps.

First, crude oil is heated and fed into an atmospheric distillation column, where it separates into fractions by boiling point — gases and petrol at the top, diesel and gas oils in the middle, and a heavy residue at the bottom. That residue is then sent to a vacuum distillation unit, where reduced pressure removes more of the remaining gas oils without overheating the material.

What remains is vacuum residue, the base for bitumen. Depending on the grade required, it may be air-blown (oxidised) to raise its softening point, or blended to meet viscosity and penetration targets set by BIS and ASTM standards.

For demanding roads, bitumen is modified further — with Kraton SBS polymers for PMB and HiMA, or with additives such as polyphosphoric acid (PPA) — to improve elasticity, rutting resistance and ageing performance.
